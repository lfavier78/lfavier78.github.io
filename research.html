Reconstruction of dronning maud land ice sheet from the last glacial maximum - Glaciologie Bruxelles

This is currently my main research with the Laboratoire de Glaciologie at ULB in Brussels. The purpose of the IceCon project I am working for is to improve our understanding of the past and present ice volumes and extension of the Antarctic Ice Sheet in Dronning Maud Land. A part of the project is dedicated to measurements and observations, and another part, in which I am mostly involved, is about ice sheet modelling.

Ice rise formation and evolution

Numerous ice rises form through the Antarctic ice shelves. They stem from the contact between a topographic high emerging from the edge of the continental shelf and the floating ice above. They increase the buttressing of ice shelves and may have a crucial role in hampering the retreat of ice sheets, hence the rise in sea level. the grounding line in pine island glacier is retreating fast - lgge

Pine Island Glacier is at present the largest single contributor to sea-level rise in Antarctica. Three state-of-the-art ice sheet models, Elmer/Ice (full Stokes model), BISICLES (Schoof-Hindmarsh model) and Uà (SSA model), were used to simulate the next 50 years of Pine Island Glacier in West Antarctica. The simulations show that the ice sheet is unstable and that the retreat which is observed today will continue until a more flat bedrock is reached by the grounding line. The outcome of the simulations is a 100 Gt/yr of ice mass loss equivalent to 3.5-10 mm eustatic sea-level rise over the following 20 years.

effect of a pinning point beneath an ice shelf from an ideal world point of vieW? - LGGE

Antarctic ice mass is essentially lost trough outlet glaciers and ice streams that feed floating ice shelves. On the edge of the continental shelf a substantial number of seamounts emerge from the edge or the continental shelf around Antarctica. Those features translate into pinning points when they attach to the otherwise freely floating ice shelves from beneath and therefore lead to buttressing and ice-shelf stability. With the current sea-level rise, some pinning points may detach from the ice bottom and lose their influence onto the ice dynamics. In this study we investigated the influence of a pinning point on the dynamics of an idealised ice sheet with the full Stokes ice sheet model Elmer/Ice. The model showed a dramatic decrease of the ice velocity immediately after the ice bottom surface starts to be pinned by a small seamount, and the grounding line subsequently advances towards the pinning point. We also performed the reverse experiment by simply removing the pinning point. The model then showed the reversibility and the ice sheet retrieved its initial state.
